User Research

Social media users come across ads and posts everyday as they scroll through news feeds. Occasionally users report ads. But what happens after they report, and how do they feel? What will make them stay vigilant?

Research Methods

User Interviews

Contextual Inquiry

Speed Dating

Semi-structured interviews were conducted to understand user's overall feelings and experiences with reporting ads in their feeds.

Directed storytelling and task analysis helped garner insights into user behavior.

We gathered feedback on 12 early concept storyboard cards relating to reporting ads.

Synthesis

Initial Hypotheses

After synthesizing our findings from initial interviews, data analysis, and background research we came up with several hypotheses related to how users interact with and experience algorithms and ads.

  • Transparency of ad targeting is poor
  • Reporting & Ad settings are poorly designed, with too many steps and opaque phrases
  • Users aren't highly motivated to report ads unless they feel strong emotions like anger
  • Regulation is underutilized and under-explored

Speed Dating Ideation

To assess how to tackle our 'how might we' problem, we each practiced a 'Crazy 8s' exercise by drawing out 8 different ideas for a solution. We then picked our favorites and compiled them into an interview guide to test out the ideas. We interviewed 4 participants from a range of backgrounds and knowledge of algorithmic bias.

We presented 12 different ideas, ranging from safe to 'risky', and asked the users to provide their thoughts and feedback.

Overview of Findings from Interviews

Users do not like automated, impersonal responses, but don’t want feedback that makes them go out of their way.

Users liked the idea of getting an automatic “thank you” response and chat-bot idea (Amazon-like customer service), but they thought getting follow up in their mailboxes would be too much. They didn’t want to have to engage with reporting outside of the platform.

There is an expectation that once you’re done submitting a report, the process ends. If there are user expectations about how many steps reporting is, that should be made clear in the beginning. Otherwise, users don’t want to be bothered again after submitting a report.


Social proof with reporting ads might not be a strong enough motivator.

Users liked the idea of knowing how many people they have impacted, but they had a hard time imagining when they would look at the stats of how many people’s news feeds were changed based on their actions. Users also had a hard time envisioning where a repository of past reporting and stats would live, and how that would integrate into their normal social media browsing habits.

Users had concerns about making the “report” button too accessible could have unintended consequences.

Having the reporting button outside of the 3 ellipses menu could cause more problems because it could lead to more reporting than necessary or accidental reporting of benign ads/content.Users said that they had a good idea of what the process funnel was for reporting processes, although it differs by platform. If they are already motivated to report, they will follow through no matter what the steps are (to an extent), so they didn’t feel strongly about having the report button more visible.

People do not react well to being listened to constantly.

In one of our “out-there” / “riskiest” idea of having an AI listener automatically report ads based on verbal feedback, people on first instinct did not react well. People do not want to be listened to as that feels like it infringes on their privacy. Users browse and react silently. There is almost never a time (across all participants) where they said they’ve reacted to an ad or content out-loud, in-the-moment.

New Design Opportunities

Better feedback for users

Timing and method are important factors about showing feedback to users. Users don’t like it when the feedback feels empty and canned, but also when the feedback is too involved, it can make some users feel like it’s extra work put on them.

Change how ad settings are shown to users

Provide better visibility of how to select and change ad & privacy settings, by providing clearer choices and adjusting the visual hierachy on the settings page to enable users to access what they need to faster and with less confusion.

A chat-bot for reporting

Enable a chatbot that supports the user and lets them know that the company is engaged and listening to their feedback.

Let users know why they are seeing something

Provide better visibility of how and why users are seeing ads. Currently, when users click 'why am I seeing this ad?', the resounding feedback was that the answers were too broad, generic and opaque. Empower the user with more information.
